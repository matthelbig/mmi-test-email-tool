Your email does not include a link to a web version

Including a link to an online/web version of your email provides subscribers with an opportunity to view your email in all its glory, even if their email client or mobile device doesn't support HTML. We may not be able to detect special tags in your HTML if your mailing service automatically creates a web/online version for you.

Yay! All your links have absolute paths

Since your email could be loaded in lots of different environments, it's important that your links have absolute paths. Subscribers may not be able to click on relative links in an email, especially if they are outside your company network. Absolute links will begin with http:// or https://.

It looks like some of your images don't have a height and width set

Here are the ones we noticed:

  1. img/logo.png
  2. img/header.jpg
  3. img/store-image.jpg

Specifying an accurate height and width on images helps ensure that your graphics display properly. Including height and width on images also helps alt text display by creating a placeholder in email clients that block images by default.

It looks like some of your image URLs have relative paths

Here are the ones we noticed:

  1. img/logo.png
  2. img/header.jpg
  3. img/store-image.jpg

Similar to relative and absolute links (above), you'll want to be sure all your images have absolute URLs, too. Your subscribers won't be able to see images in your email that are linked with relative paths. Since we're unable to upload your images, you'll want to host your images elsewhere before performing this check. If you upload a zip file of HTML and images to your mailing service, they may create absolute paths for you.

Woo! All your images have alt attributes

Since many email clients block images by default, it's likely that your email will be viewed frequently with images "turned off". Using alt attributes to describe the images in your email allows recipients to understand your message even when they can't see the images.

Some email clients (iPhone, Gmail, Outlook for example) will display the first few lines of text from the body of your email alongside the subject line.
